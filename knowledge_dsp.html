Discrete-Time Signals

Vectors, Impulses, and Mathematical Manipulations (Week 1)

🔢

Sequence Representation

Digital signals are purely mathematical sequences of numbers plotted against a discrete time index `n`.

x[n] = { 1.5, 2.0, -1.0, 0.5 }
📌

The Impulse Function δ[n]

The delta/impulse function is 1 precisely at n=0, and 0 everywhere else. All other signals can be formed by summing shifted impulses.

δ[n] = 1 for n=0, else 0
⏪

Signal Delay

Shifting a signal mathematically. A delay pushes the signal into the future (shifts graphic RIGHT by k samples).

y[n] = x[n - k]

🔄 Signal Formatting Examples

  • Exponential Decay: x[n] = (0.5)^n . Because the base (0.5) is less than 1, the signal physically decays exponentially towards 0 over time.
  • Time Scaling: x[-n] mathematically reverses the signal around the Y-axis. x[2n] compresses the signal, discarding every odd sample.

📝 Worked Example: Linear Discrete Convolution

Convolution mathematically describes how an input signal \( x[n] \) interacts with a system's impulse response \( h[n] \) to produce the output \( y[n] \).

\[ y[n] = x[n] * h[n] = \sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty} x[k] h[n-k] \]

Problem:

Calculate the linear convolution of the input \( x[n] = [1, 2] \) and the impulse response \( h[n] = [3, -1] \). Assume the first sequence member is at \( n=0 \).

Solution (The Slide-and-Multiply Matrix):

\[ \begin{array}{c|cc} & x[0]=1 & x[1]=2 \\ \hline h[0]=3 & 3 & 6 \\ h[1]=-1 & -1 & -2 \end{array} \]

Summing the diagonals mathematically yields the final output array \( y[n] \):

  • \( y[0] = 3 \)
  • \( y[1] = 6 + (-1) = 5 \)
  • \( y[2] = -2 \)

Final Answer: \( y[n] = [ 3, 5, -2 ] \)

A/D Conversion & Sampling

Extracting discrete points from continuous analog mediums.

The Normalized Frequency

In analog, frequency is Hz (cycles per second). In digital, time is stripped away, leaving only "samples". We normalize frequency to radians per sample.

ω = 2π * (F / Fs)

Fs is your Sampling Frequency. The highest mathematically representable angular frequency in DSP is ω = π.

Periodicity of Sinusoids

In the analog world, ALL sine waves are periodic. But in Discrete DSP, a digital sine wave is ONLY periodic if its frequency (F/Fs) is a pure rational number.

N = 2π / ω * k

If the result N is an integer, the sequence is perfectly periodic repeating every N samples.

👻

The Aliasing Phenomenon

What happens when you sample too slowly!

📉 Nyquist-Shannon Sampling Theorem

To perfectly reconstruct an analog signal without artifacts, you MUST sample the signal at a frequency greater than TWICE its highest frequency component.

Fs > 2 * F_max
⚠️

Folding / Aliasing Collisions

If you fail the Nyquist condition (e.g. sampling a 170Hz wave at exactly 200Hz), the high frequency wave mathematically "folds" back over the spectrum and masquerades as a low-frequency wave. The computer will incorrectly interpret the 170Hz wave as a 30Hz ghost signal!

Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT & FFT) Take a closer look..

The DFT converts a finite sequence of equally-spaced samples into a same-length sequence of complex numbers (frequency bins). However, manually computing the Direct Matrix form is computationally devastating for a CPU.

Direct Computation \( O(N^2) \)

  • Multiplications: \( N^2 \) complex multiplications.
  • Additions: \( N(N-1) \) complex additions.
  • For a 1024-point sequence (\(N=1024\)), direct computation demands over 1 Million explicit multiplications!

Fast Fourier Transform \( O(N \log_2 N) \)

  • The FFT is not a new equation! It's just a hyper-efficient algorithmic trick (divide-and-conquer) to compute the exact DFT.
  • Multiplications: \( \frac{N}{2} \log_2 N \) multiplications.
  • Additions: \( N \log_2 N \) additions.
  • For \(N=1024\), the FFT reduces 1,000,000 multiplications to just 5,120!
The Z-Transform defined Take a closer look..

The Z-Transform perfectly maps a discrete-time signal \( x[n] \) into the complex frequency domain (the Z-plane). It is functionally equivalent to the Laplace transform used in continuous-time physics.

\[ X(z) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} x[n] z^{-n} \]
  • Time Delay Theorem: Physically delaying a signal \( x[n-k] \) perfectly equates to multiplying the Z-domain representation by \( z^{-k} \).
  • Convolution Theorem: Convolution in the time domain precisely equals strict multiplication in the Z-domain: \( y[n] = x[n] * h[n] \implies Y(z) = X(z)H(z) \).

📝 Worked Example: Deriving the Unit Step \( u[n] \)

Examiners frequently ask you to mathematically derive the Z-Transform of the standard unit step function, \( u[n] \) (where \( x[n] = 1 \) for all \( n \ge 0 \), and \( 0 \) otherwise).

\[ \begin{aligned} X(z) &= \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} u[n] z^{-n} \\ X(z) &= \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} (1) z^{-n} \\ X(z) &= \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} (z^{-1})^n \end{aligned} \]

This is a perfect endless geometric series of the form \( \sum a r^n \). The infinite sum absolutely converges to \( \frac{1}{1-r} \) strictly if and only if \( |r| < 1 \).

\[ X(z) = \frac{1}{1 - z^{-1}}, \quad \text{ROC: } |z^{-1}| < 1 \implies |z| > 1 \]

Final Answer: \( \frac{z}{z - 1} \) with a region of convergence stretching outward infinitely from the pole at \( z = 1 \).

Difference Equations Take a closer look..

Digital systems are implemented universally via linear constant-coefficient difference equations linking past and current inputs \( x[n] \) to outputs \( y[n] \).

\[ \sum_{k=0}^{N} a_k y[n-k] = \sum_{m=0}^{M} b_m x[n-m] \]

Assuming \( a_0 = 1 \), the current system output physically computes as:

\[ y[n] = \sum_{m=0}^{M} b_m x[n-m] - \sum_{k=1}^{N} a_k y[n-k] \]

📝 Worked Example: IIR System Output

Given the difference equation \( y[n] = x[n] + 0.5 y[n-1] \), calculate the output \( y[n] \) for \( n = 0, 1, 2 \). Assume the system is perfectly relaxed (meaning \( y[-1] = 0 \)) and your input is an impulse \( x[n] = \delta[n] \).

  • At n = 0:
    \( y[0] = x[0] + 0.5 y[-1] \)
    \( y[0] = 1 + 0.5(0) \implies 1 \)
  • At n = 1:
    \( y[1] = x[1] + 0.5 y[0] \)
    \( y[1] = 0 + 0.5(1) \implies 0.5 \)
  • At n = 2:
    \( y[2] = x[2] + 0.5 y[1] \)
    \( y[2] = 0 + 0.5(0.5) \implies 0.25 \)

Examiner Observation: Even though the input signal explicitly died completely after \( n=0 \), the output continues endlessly calculating forever! This is mathematically why it is an Infinite Impulse Response.

FIR vs IIR Filter Systems Take a closer look..

There are two fundamental topologies physically employed in discrete filter engineering.

Finite Impulse Response (FIR)

  • Equation restricts strictly to inputs: \( y[n] = \sum b_m x[n-m] \)
  • Absolutely No Feedback: It strictly cannot feed outputs back into itself.
  • Condition: Unconditionally mathematically stable.

Infinite Impulse Response (IIR)

  • Employs violent mathematical feedback loops: \( a_k y[n-k] \)
  • Poles: Because of feedback, the denominator of its transfer function \( H(z) \) possesses roots (poles).
  • Condition: The filter is only strictly physically stable if ALL mathematical poles exist entirely inside the Unit Circle: \( |z| < 1 \).

Graphic: IIR Feedback Loop (Direct Form)

The aggressive red feedback loop explicitly ties past outputs back into the arithmetic adder. This exact feedback mechanism generates poles and creates the Infinite Impulse Response.

Pole-Zero Analysis Take a closer look..

Evaluating the transfer function \( H(z) = Y(z) / X(z) \):

\[ H(z) = \frac{\sum b_m z^{-m}}{\sum a_k z^{-k}} \implies H(z) = \frac{N(z)}{D(z)} \]
  • Zeros \( (o) \): Roots of the numerator \( N(z) \). Whenever \( z \) equals a zero, the system completely annihilates that frequency.
  • Poles \( (x) \): Roots of the denominator \( D(z) \). Whenever \( z \) identically equals a pole, the system response mathematically explodes towards infinity.

Examiner's Stability Trap

For a causal system, stability is strictly determined by poles. Zeros have absolutely no bearing on stability!

  • If a pole is at \( |z| < 1 \) (inside the circle), its impulse response decays \(\to\) Stable.
  • If a pole is at \( |z| > 1 \) (outside the circle), its impulse response explodes \(\to\) Unstable.
  • The ROC mathematically MUST include the unit circle \( |z| = 1 \).

🔍 Examiner's Geometric Z-Plane Traps

1. Complex Conjugate Law

If a filter has real coefficients, all complex poles MUST securely exist in mirror pairs across the Real axis.

2. Reciprocal Symmetry

For an FIR filter to have perfect Linear Phase, every zero \(z_0\) must have a reciprocal twin at \(1/z_0\).

3. Minimum Phase

A system is Minimum Phase if and only if ALL poles AND ALL ZEROS are strictly locked inside the Unit Circle.

Practical Filter Design & Windows Take a closer look..

Ideal filters intuitively possess a perfect "Brick-Wall" drop-off, seamlessly transitioning from passband to stopband. In physical reality, this necessitates an infinite impulse response duration! Practical filters therefore exhibit physical imperfections.

Graphic: Anatomy of a Practical Low-Pass Filter

Windowing Functions

  • Rectangular Window: Simplest truncation. Yields the narrowest transition band, but causes violent high-amplitude ripples (Gibbs Phenomenon).
  • Hamming/Hanning Window: Tapers off smoothly. Massively suppresses side-lobe ripple amplitude, at the direct expense of a wider Transition Band width.

Parks-McClellan algorithm

  • Utilizes the Remez exchange algorithm to design optimal FIR filters.
  • Equiripple: Distributes the error evenly across the passband and stopband.
  • The "Don't Care" Band: PM explicitly ignores optimizing the Transition Band, focusing math entirely on maximizing pass/stop behavior!
