Discrete-Time Signals

Vectors, Impulses, and Mathematical Manipulations (Week 1)

🔢

Sequence Representation

Digital signals are purely mathematical sequences of numbers plotted against a discrete time index `n`.

x[n] = { 1.5, 2.0, -1.0, 0.5 }
📌

The Impulse Function δ[n]

The delta/impulse function is 1 precisely at n=0, and 0 everywhere else. All other signals can be formed by summing shifted impulses.

δ[n] = 1 for n=0, else 0
⏪

Signal Delay

Shifting a signal mathematically. A delay pushes the signal into the future (shifts graphic RIGHT by k samples).

y[n] = x[n - k]

🔄 Signal Formatting Examples

  • Exponential Decay: x[n] = (0.5)^n . Because the base (0.5) is less than 1, the signal physically decays exponentially towards 0 over time.
  • Time Scaling: x[-n] mathematically reverses the signal around the Y-axis. x[2n] compresses the signal, discarding every odd sample.

A/D Conversion & Sampling

Extracting discrete points from continuous analog mediums.

The Normalized Frequency

In analog, frequency is Hz (cycles per second). In digital, time is stripped away, leaving only "samples". We normalize frequency to radians per sample.

ω = 2π * (F / Fs)

Fs is your Sampling Frequency. The highest mathematically representable angular frequency in DSP is ω = π.

Periodicity of Sinusoids

In the analog world, ALL sine waves are periodic. But in Discrete DSP, a digital sine wave is ONLY periodic if its frequency (F/Fs) is a pure rational number.

N = 2π / ω * k

If the result N is an integer, the sequence is perfectly periodic repeating every N samples.

👻

The Aliasing Phenomenon

What happens when you sample too slowly!

📉 Nyquist-Shannon Sampling Theorem

To perfectly reconstruct an analog signal without artifacts, you MUST sample the signal at a frequency greater than TWICE its highest frequency component.

Fs > 2 * F_max
⚠️

Folding / Aliasing Collisions

If you fail the Nyquist condition (e.g. sampling a 170Hz wave at exactly 200Hz), the high frequency wave mathematically "folds" back over the spectrum and masquerades as a low-frequency wave. The computer will incorrectly interpret the 170Hz wave as a 30Hz ghost signal!
